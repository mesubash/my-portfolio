# Why I Write

I'm a software engineer. I build systems, debug code, and think in abstractions. So why do I spend time writing essays that have nothing to do with APIs or databases?

Because writing is thinking. And thinking clearly is the most important engineering skill there is.

## Writing as Debugging Thought

When I write code, I'm translating intent into instructions. The compiler tells me when my logic is wrong. The tests tell me when my assumptions are broken. The system gives me feedback.

Writing prose works the same way, except *I* am the compiler. When I try to explain an idea in writing and it doesn't make sense on the page, it means it doesn't make sense in my head either. The act of writing forces me to confront the gaps in my understanding.

I've lost count of the number of times I've started writing about a technical decision and realized halfway through that I couldn't justify it. That's not a failure of writing — it's writing doing exactly what it should.

## The Engineering-Writing Connection

The best engineers I know are also excellent communicators. Not because communication is a "soft skill" bolted onto their technical ability, but because it's the same skill:

- **Clarity**: Good code is readable. Good writing is clear.
- **Structure**: Good architecture has clear boundaries. Good essays have clear arguments.
- **Empathy**: Good APIs consider the developer experience. Good writing considers the reader.
- **Simplicity**: Good systems avoid unnecessary complexity. Good writing avoids unnecessary words.

## What I Write About

I write about whatever I'm thinking about:

- **Engineering**: Architecture decisions, debugging war stories, technology deep dives
- **Philosophy**: How I think about work, meaning, and growth
- **Personal**: Lessons from my journey, reflections on life in Kathmandu
- **Projects**: What I built, why, and what I learned

The category doesn't matter as much as the practice. Every piece of writing makes the next one easier, clearer, more honest.

## The Public Practice

Publishing is different from writing. Writing is thinking; publishing is sharing that thinking with others. It's scarier, but it's also more valuable.

When I publish, I commit to a standard. I can't hand-wave or leave ideas half-formed. Someone might read this. Someone might disagree. Someone might learn something. That accountability makes the writing better.

> "Writing is nature's way of letting you know how sloppy your thinking is." — Dick Guindon

## An Invitation

If you're an engineer who doesn't write, try it. Not for an audience — for yourself. Write about what you're learning, what you're building, what you're struggling with.

You'll find that the practice of writing makes you a better thinker, a better communicator, and — yes — a better engineer.

That's why I write.
